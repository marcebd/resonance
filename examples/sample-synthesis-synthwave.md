## Divergent Reactions

**Variant B — THE SCANNER DARKLY — Leandro (advocate) vs Gitte (detractor)**
> Variant B is the most polarized track in the test (variance 6.22, range 2-9) and the disagreement is structural, not aesthetic: it rewards listeners who read buried melody and atonal stabs as compositional menace, and punishes listeners who need melodic resolution to decompress. Leandro called it "genuinamente inquietante" and the rare track that escapes decorative darkness; Gitte literally turned it off mid-bike-ride because it raised her heart rate. This is a track that works hard for its core audience precisely by failing the adjacent one — meaning B has the sharpest identity of any variant tested but the narrowest viable distribution context.

**Variant E — THE GRID COMMUNION — Renata (advocate) vs Leandro (detractor)**
> Variant E (variance 4.14, range 3-9) splits along a different axis: scene-purist listeners experience its motorik propulsion as "daytime synthwave" that betrays the brief, while non-scene and visuals-driven listeners experience the same forward momentum as the most emotionally legible track of the set. Renata scored it 9 because the locked grid let her stay in flow; Leandro scored it 3 because the same locked grid read to him as "esteira de academia" — gym treadmill — with the danger mechanically removed. The disagreement reveals E is genre-adjacent rather than genre-core, which matters for placement.

## Recommendation

Variant D wins the aggregate by a marginal 0.07 over Variant A — that gap is statistical noise, not a verdict, so **treat D and A as a co-leading pair rather than a clear winner**. The more useful signal is qualitative: D is the only variant that earned high marks from both canon-literate insiders (Tomasz and Leandro at 9) AND mood-driven focus listeners (Renata at 8, Yejin at 7), suggesting its restraint translates across listening contexts in a way the louder variants don't.

Recommend **advancing Variant D as the lead candidate** for nighttime-driving playlist seeding and **pairing it with Variant B** as a deliberate "deep cut" release for scene-core channels where polarization is an asset rather than a risk; **hold Variant E** and **rework Variant C's** mid-track sparse section before any further testing.

Critically, these findings are **directional, not predictive** — synthetic personas approximate reaction patterns but cannot account for platform algorithm behavior, real listener fatigue curves, or the contextual cues (artwork, playlist adjacency, time-of-day surfacing) that drive actual save and skip rates. Validate D against a real-audience A/B test in a driving-context playlist before committing distribution spend, and specifically probe whether D's intentional empty bars survive a passive-listening environment where Yejin's "I can't hum it back" concern could become a retention problem at scale.
