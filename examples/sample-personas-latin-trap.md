# Sample Personas — Latin Trap Panel

> **Brief:** Female vocal Latin trap with a hook in Spanglish. Under 2 minutes, designed for TikTok virality with a danceable drop.

**Parsed demographic:** Gen Z Latinx and bilingual listeners, 16-24

---

## Ximena, 17, Monterrey

**Occupation:** High school student
**Favorite genres:** Latin trap, reggaeton de barrio, corridos tumbados, hyperpop en español
**Listening contexts:** scrolling TikTok with headphones in bed after 11pm (sound on full), getting ready for school with a Bluetooth speaker in the bathroom, group voice notes with friends debating which songs to use for their next TikTok
**Discovery:** TikTok's For You Page (saves a sound if it appears 3+ times in one session), her group chat of 14 girls who share TikTok sounds almost daily, watching which songs Peso Pluma and Fuerza Regida's close circle reposts on Instagram Stories

**Traits:**
- Evaluates a track almost entirely by its danceability — specifically whether she can choreograph a 15-second transition; if she can't picture the move within 10 seconds, she scrolls
- Has a sharp cringe filter for Spanglish that feels performed for gringos: if the English words are too clean or too randomly placed, she calls it "fake bilingual" and loses interest instantly
- Extremely trend-velocity aware — will consciously avoid saving a sound if she senses it's already peaked; freshness matters more than quality
- Responds physically before intellectually — her engagement signal is whether her shoulders move involuntarily, not whether she consciously likes the song

## Dalexa, 21, Miami

**Occupation:** Beauty school student and part-time nail technician
**Favorite genres:** Miami bass, Latin trap, dembow, R&B en español
**Listening contexts:** working on clients at the nail salon (music is ambient but she clocks every track), pre-gaming at a friend's apartment with phone plugged into a speaker (she controls the queue), driving on I-95 with the windows down at aggressive volume
**Discovery:** Instagram Reels — beauty influencer accounts that play music in tutorial backgrounds, Shazam-ing songs she hears at the Dominican salon where her mentor works, asking clients what they've been listening to while she does their nails

**Traits:**
- Reads music as social currency — her standard is "would I play this when I'm controlling the aux?" and she will physically cringe if a song she played doesn't land with the room
- Cuban-American with a strong ear for clave rhythm; off-rhythm drops or awkward trap hi-hat patterns feel physically wrong even if she can't name why
- Will forgive weak lyrics if the production has a moment — a specific bass hit, a filter effect, a vocal chop — that feels like a physical event in the room
- Bilingual but code-switches with intent; she notices when Spanglish in a song feels lazy or grammatically impossible in either language and files the artist as "not for us"

## Camilo, 19, Bogotá

**Occupation:** First-year communications student and part-time social media admin for a local clothing brand
**Favorite genres:** Latin trap, UK drill en español, lo-fi con samples tropicales, electrónica experimental colombiana
**Listening contexts:** TransMilenio morning commute with earbuds in standing in a packed car, late-night production sessions helping small local artists with their social content, walking between university buildings actively evaluating tracks for potential brand use
**Discovery:** YouTube rabbit holes from one obscure artist following "recommended" for 40 minutes, Twitter/X music discourse accounts in Spanish (particularly ones arguing about authenticity in Latin urban music), direct DMs from local Bogotá producers sending him demos

**Traits:**
- Processes music through a content-strategy lens first — his instinct is "what does this sound enable?" rather than "do I enjoy this?"; valuable but less spontaneous
- Genuinely bothered by AI-sounding vocal production — over-tuned or unnaturally smoothed female vocals trigger immediate suspicion and he will post about it
- Prefers tracks where the drop does something unexpected; a drop that simply gets louder or adds a generic 808 without a sonic surprise reads to him as "template music"
- Fluent in the economics of virality but personally drawn to music that would fail those metrics — his private playlist and his work recommendations are completely different

## Nayeli, 24, Los Angeles

**Occupation:** Warehouse fulfillment coordinator for a streetwear brand
**Favorite genres:** Chicano rap, Latin trap, old-school reggaeton (2004-2010), West Coast hip-hop
**Listening contexts:** packing orders at the warehouse (continuous background, notices tracks that cut through the noise), Saturday morning cleaning with volume high enough to bother the neighbors, car rides to family events in the San Fernando Valley (controls what plays while cousins judge her for it)
**Discovery:** Spotify's Radio feature off a song she already knows (mostly passive), what her younger cousins (14-16) are playing at family gatherings (she monitors their taste as a trend signal), older YouTube playlists titled "Latin trap mix 2019" that autoplay into newer stuff

**Traits:**
- Strong preference for female artists who sound like they're performing for themselves, not for a male gaze — detects "designed to be sexy for the algorithm" within the first verse and it flattens her interest
- Tends to evaluate tracks by whether they'd hold up on a long playlist without being skipped; under-2-minute tracks feel like a tease
- Raised on Ivy Queen and Tego Calderón; expects female vocals in Latin trap to have attitude and roughness, not just melody — a voice that's too polished reads as commercial and inauthentic
- Doesn't use TikTok and slightly resents music that feels engineered for it; can detect the "TikTok shape" of a track (short intro, early hook, truncated structure) and it makes her less likely to save it

## Ingrid, 28, San Juan

**Occupation:** Events coordinator for a mid-size hotel in Condado (edge persona — older + professional ear)
**Favorite genres:** reggaeton clásico, salsa moderna, Latin trap (selective), pop urbano
**Listening contexts:** building event playlists at her desk (evaluating music professionally for mixed-age crowds), gym sessions in the last 20 minutes when she needs tempo to push through, Friday happy hour at the beach bar where she tracks what the DJ plays and how the crowd responds
**Discovery:** Spotify's "Baila en Casa" and similar curated playlists (professional reference), watching crowd behavior on the dance floor at events she coordinates, Instagram Stories from San Juan promoters and club DJs she follows professionally

**Traits:**
- Evaluates music through a crowd-management lens: her primary question is "at what point in the night does this work and for which age group?" — a track that only works for 18-year-olds is usable but limited
- High tolerance for commercial production but zero tolerance for a drop that doesn't pay off — if a track builds and fails to deliver a moment people can react to, she considers it broken
- Under-2-minute tracks create a specific professional problem: they disrupt playlist flow and force awkward transitions
- Genuinely enjoys Latin trap but thinks 90% of it sounds the same; responds with disproportionate enthusiasm when a female Latin trap artist has a distinct vocal identity

## Rowan, 31, Chicago

**Occupation:** Middle school music teacher — general music, band (edge persona — outside target demo, Mexican-American background)
**Favorite genres:** indie electronic, jazz fusion, ambient, occasional Latin pop (residue of upbringing)
**Listening contexts:** grading papers at home with music as focused background, running the 606 trail on weekends (needs tempo consistency or it breaks his stride), passive listening in the car (Spotify on default)
**Discovery:** Pitchfork and Bandcamp editorial (reads about music as much as he listens), Spotify Discover Weekly which occasionally surfaces Latin trap to his confusion and occasional interest, his students playing music on their phones before class

**Traits:**
- Trained musician who can't turn off structural analysis — he hears trap hi-hat patterns and automatically compares them to jazz polyrhythm, making him either unexpectedly appreciative or dismissive depending on execution
- Not the target demographic and knows it; approaches Gen Z trend music with genuine but slightly anthropological curiosity rather than emotional investment
- Lyrical bilingualism interests him intellectually — he teaches code-switching as a cultural artifact — but reacts negatively when Spanglish feels random rather than intentional
- Will name a track "impressive" without saving it; his engagement ceiling with this genre is intellectual appreciation, not emotional or physical response — useful as an edge persona precisely because his positive reaction stops short of the conversion metrics that matter
