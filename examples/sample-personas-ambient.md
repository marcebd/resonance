# Sample Personas — Ambient Piano Panel

> **Brief:** Ambient piano with soft pads, no vocals, very slow. For sleep playlists. 40-60 BPM.

**Parsed demographic:** adults 25-45 seeking sleep or relaxation aids

---

## Lieselotte, 27, Amsterdam

**Occupation:** UX researcher at a health-tech startup
**Favorite genres:** ambient, modern classical, drone, lowercase
**Listening contexts:** falling asleep with headphones in (uses music as a literal sleep trigger, not background), late-night reading sessions with the lamp on low, weekend afternoon naps after long runs
**Discovery:** The Quietus and Fluid Radio editorial features, Bandcamp rabbit-holing through artist recommendations on label pages, word of mouth from a small Signal group with four other ambient obsessives

**Traits:**
- Listens to sleep music with active, evaluative attention before sleep takes over — she will notice a jarring harmonic resolution or a pad that fades too abruptly and mentally log it as a flaw
- Deeply suspicious of piano-led ambient that leans cinematic; if a piece starts to feel like a film score or emotional cue, she checks out — she wants texture, not narrative arc
- Has a long-running personal playlist she curates obsessively and adds new tracks to only after a three-listen trial — most tracks fail the third listen
- Genuinely unbothered by silence and long decay tails; will reward a track that trusts its own space rather than padding transitions

## Tremaine, 34, Atlanta

**Occupation:** ER nurse, rotating 12-hour night shifts
**Favorite genres:** R&B, neo-soul, lo-fi hip-hop, ambient
**Listening contexts:** decompressing alone in the car in the hospital parking garage for 15-20 minutes before driving home after a shift, trying to fall asleep at 9 AM with blackout curtains and a white noise fan already running, passive background while meal-prepping on days off
**Discovery:** Spotify Sleep and Focus algorithm playlists (almost never seeks music actively, trusts the playlist to serve him), occasionally follows a recommendation from a coworker who texts him Spotify links, YouTube autoplay from lo-fi hip-hop streams

**Traits:**
- Purely functional relationship with sleep music — does not care about artist, label, or genre; if it works as a sleep aid it stays in rotation; if it doesn't, he skips without a second thought
- Hypersensitive to dynamic spikes: a single note that peaks louder than the ambient wash will snap him out of the pre-sleep state, and he will not replay the track
- Has no patience for music that "tries too hard to be pretty" — overly melodic or emotionally legible piano lines feel manipulative after a shift where he has been emotionally saturated all day
- Will never look up what a track is called; his engagement with music is entirely passive and context-dependent

## Ysolde, 41, Lyon

**Occupation:** Secondary school music teacher (piano and theory)
**Favorite genres:** contemporary classical, impressionist piano, ambient neoclassical, field recording
**Listening contexts:** deliberate evening listening after her daughters are in bed (sits in a chair with no phone and just listens), early morning before the house wakes (with coffee, as a kind of secular ritual), occasionally as background while grading student compositions
**Discovery:** ECM Records new releases and back catalog exploration, French radio programme France Musique for contemporary classical features, recommendations from a colleague who runs a small concert series

**Traits:**
- Trained pianist who involuntarily analyzes voicing and pedaling in any piano piece — will immediately notice lazy sustain pedal use that muddies a chord, and it will diminish the track even in a sleep context
- Values harmonic ambiguity over resolution; a track that resolves too cleanly onto a tonic feels juvenile and breaks the meditative state
- Forgiving of minimalism to a degree most listeners are not — she can sit with a track that barely moves for six minutes if the timbral detail repays attention
- Resistant to music she suspects was AI-generated, though she cannot always identify it — her instinct flags tracks that have "correct" voicing but lack a felt sense of physical hand weight on the keys

## Kofi, 29, Accra

**Occupation:** Freelance motion graphics designer
**Favorite genres:** afrofusion, electronic ambient, jazz, chillwave
**Listening contexts:** long late-night work sessions with headphones on while rendering video files, commuting by trotro with earbuds in (uses music to create mental separation from the noise of the city), falling asleep after finishing a project deadline (usually past 2 AM)
**Discovery:** TikTok ambient/study content (saves sounds from "working at 3am" videos and hunts down the full track), YouTube "music to work to" compilations and the comment sections where people name the tracks, occasionally Apple Music editorial playlists

**Traits:**
- Listens to ambient music instrumentally — it must serve his work state; if a track pulls his attention away from the screen because it does something interesting, that is a failure condition
- Drawn to textural contrast: appreciates when pads have subtle movement or grain rather than sitting completely flat — pure stillness can feel sterile rather than calming
- Has no emotional or intellectual investment in "authentic" musicianship; produced pad, sample, generative — all fine; what matters is how it lands in his ears right now
- Will add a track to a dedicated "late work" playlist after one listen if it passes, never revisits it as a standalone piece — high save rate, zero replay-as-focused-listening rate

## Bríd, 44, Galway

**Occupation:** Cognitive behavioural therapist in private practice
**Favorite genres:** ambient neoclassical, traditional Irish folk, slow chamber music, nature sound recordings
**Listening contexts:** playing music quietly through a Bluetooth speaker in her therapy room as ambient backdrop before sessions, her own wind-down routine (bath, then 20 minutes of music in a dark room before sleep), long walks on the coast with earbuds (using music to extend a meditative walking state)
**Discovery:** recommendations from colleagues and clients (actively asks clients what they use for sleep and sometimes tries it herself), Spotify Sleep/Calm editorial playlists (which she also recommends to clients and therefore evaluates with a professional ear), occasional podcast features on the neuroscience of music and sleep

**Traits:**
- Evaluates sleep music with a quasi-professional lens — she thinks about whether a track would help a client with hyperarousal sleep difficulties, paying attention to predictability, absence of sudden change, and emotional neutrality
- Actively dislikes music that evokes grief or loss even subtly — a minor-key resolution or a descending phrase that lingers can feel mournful, and she won't use it in clinical or personal sleep contexts
- Tolerates repetition more than almost any other listener type — looping or slow cycling of a motif reads as soothing rather than boring
- Skeptical of tracks under four minutes for sleep use; she considers anything shorter than five minutes insufficiently immersive for an actual sleep onset function

## Ryszard, 52, Wrocław

**Occupation:** Civil engineer at a municipal infrastructure firm (edge persona — well outside target, ambient via daughter only)
**Favorite genres:** classic rock, progressive rock, jazz fusion, occasionally ambient when his daughter plays it
**Listening contexts:** driving alone on long regional work trips (active listening in the car), half-listening while watching football with the sound down on a second screen, rarely: his daughter puts on ambient music during family Sunday dinners and he ends up passively exposed
**Discovery:** a Spotify account his daughter set up for him (mostly replays artists he already knows), Polish radio (occasionally catches something new), his daughter sending him links to tracks she thinks he might tolerate — genuinely his only ambient discovery pathway

**Traits:**
- Edge persona — sits well outside the target; encounters ambient music almost exclusively through his daughter and has no framework for evaluating it on its own terms; reactions will flag where the track fails to communicate to a listener without ambient literacy
- Expects music to "do something" within the first 30 seconds — not a hook exactly, but some sense of direction or event; a track that hasn't established any identity by then reads as broken or not yet loaded
- Not hostile to slow music in principle — he appreciates slow jazz and long prog suites — but his bar for paying continued attention is whether the music seems to be going somewhere, even slowly
- Will not save, playlist, or revisit; his sole output is a binary verdict — "this is nice, put it on again sometime" or complete indifference — both meaningful data points for a sleep track seeking passive acceptance from non-core listeners
